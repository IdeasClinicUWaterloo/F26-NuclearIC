System 01

Dye concentration control

PID on measured dye concentration drives a dye pump and a water (diluent) pump; a drain pump runs feed-forward off their combined flow to prevent tank overflow. Each pump gets its own solderless driver module on a direct PWM pin — no motor shield, no I2C addressing to configure.

Wiring steps

  1. Wire TCS34725: VIN→5V, GND→GND, SDA→A4, SCL→A5. Buy a version with the pin header already soldered on (standard for Adafruit boards) so there's nothing to solder yourself.
  2. For each of the 3 pumps, take one driver module: SIG→ its Arduino pin (D5 dye, D6 water, D3 drain), VCC→5V, GND→GND.
  3. Screw the dye pump's two leads into that module's load terminals; repeat for water and drain pumps on their own modules.
  4. Screw all three modules' supply-side terminals into the shared 3V pump supply (and its ground into the common ground) — not the Arduino's 5V rail.
  5. Position the TCS34725 so it reads through the mixing tank wall (or via a dip tube) where liquid color is representative of the mixed contents, not right at an inlet stream.
  6. Flash dye_concentration_controller.ino, open Serial Monitor at 9600 baud, and confirm sensor + PID debug lines are printing before connecting pump tubing.
Calibrate before trusting it Flow curves for all three pumps, the sensor's clear-water reference reading, and the concentration-vs-reading curve are placeholders in code — see the calibration notes at the bottom of the sketch.

System 02

Temperature control

The heater is not wired to the Arduino at all — it runs at constant power off its own 12V supply through a fuse, like a fixed heat source. PID only controls the coolant pump's flow through the copper coil. One actuator, one solderless driver module, and the trickiest high-current circuit in the whole project is gone.

Pin & component map

SignalPinTo
Sensor dataD2DS18B20 (+ 4.7kΩ pull-up to 5V)
Pump controlD9 (PWM)Driver module → 3V coolant pump

Power domains

Logic5VArduino USB / barrel jack

Coolant pump3Vinto driver module's screw terminal

Wiring steps

  1. Wire DS18B20: VDD→5V, GND→GND, DATA→D2, plus a 4.7kΩ resistor bridging DATA to 5V. A waterproof probe version with pre-attached wires needs no board handling at all.
  2. Take one solderless driver module for the coolant pump: SIG→D9, VCC→5V, GND→GND. Screw the pump's leads into its load terminal, and the 3V supply into its power terminal.
  3. Heater — wire this one completely separately from the Arduino: 12V+ supply → inline 15A fuse → heater(+); heater(−) → 12V supply ground. Add a simple manual switch inline if you want an easy way to turn it off by hand. No MOSFET, no driver module, no Arduino pin.
  4. Tie Arduino GND, the pump driver module's GND, the DS18B20's GND, and the 3V supply's ground to one common point. The heater's 12V circuit does not need to share this ground.
  5. Before trusting this design, bench-test cooling capacity: run the heater at full power with the pump at max speed and confirm temperature plateaus well below a safe limit rather than climbing indefinitely. If it doesn't plateau, the coil/reservoir can't keep up.
  6. Flash temperature_controller.ino, watch Serial Monitor at 9600 baud, and confirm sane temperature readings before turning the heater on.
Non-negotiable safety items 15A fuse in series with the heater's 12V feed, always — it's the only thing protecting that circuit now that it isn't software-controlled. Confirm the cooling-capacity bench test above before leaving the system running unattended.
